AnantU Establishes Two More COVID-19 Recovery Facilities in Kerala

Advaita Jairam, B.Des. | Saipriya Yellayi, B.Des.

Anant National University has set up two more COVID-19 recovery facilities in Thiruvananthapuram, Kerala, in partnership with the Government of Kerala and the Parliamentarians with Innovators for India (PI India), and collaboration with Dr Shashi Tharoor, Anil Anthony, and Cisco.

The first recovery facility, 10th in the country, is a 30-bed fully equipped recovery facility, located in a large conference hall at the Panchkarma Ayurveda Institute in Thiruvananthapuram, is phase 1 of a 100-bed project to treat patients with moderate symptoms of COVID-19. The second recovery facility, 11th in the country, is located at the Cordova Senior Secondary School in Thiruvananthapuram, which is equipped with 70 of AnantU’s proprietary beds. Besides the medical equipment, the facility has laminated corrugated cardboard beds and tables, designed and manufactured by AnantU, which are affordable and durable.

With the latest feat, led by Dr Miniya Chatterji, Director of the Anant Centre for Sustainability and Anant Fellowship for Climate Action, AnantU has made provision of nearly 1700 beds across five states in India including Delhi, Maharashtra, Kerala, Karnataka and Gujarat.

Dr Shashi Tharoor, author and Member of Parliament, lauded Anant National University's initiative of setting up the recovery facility. Dr Tharoor took to social media platforms and extended his gratitude to AnantU's design team and Dr Miniya Chatterji, Director, Centre for Sustainability, Anant National University, for coming up with a quick and affordable solution for the people amidst the ongoing pandemic.

Besides setting up recovery centres, AnantU also designed and manufactured the first-of-its-kind Anant COVID testing facilities and oxygen ambulances inside auto rickshaws to facilitate rapid testing and assisting patients during emergencies. AnantU is thus providing highly affordable, scalable, and sustainable solutions by mobilizing its in-house designers, urban planning innovators, faculty and students to help India strengthen its defence in this pandemic.

Dr Abhishek Kumar's Paper on Humanistic Leadership Published in Emerald Journal

Advaita Jairam, B.Des | Deep Tak, B.Des.

AnantU faculty and Communication & Marketing Department Head, Dr Abhishek Kumar’s paper on ‘Humanistic leadership in the TATA Group: The Synergy in Personal Values, Organisational Strategy and National Cultural Ethos’ was recently published in the prestigious ‘Emerald Journal-Cross Cultural and Strategic Management’. The paper was co-authored with Professor Ritu Tripathi of IIM Bangalore.

The paper explained how TATA Group has embodied humanistic leadership in its various levels of organisation. The paper defines these humanistic leadership values from a cultural perspective. It explores these values and translates its principles into work ethics and how they have been set up in the corporate setting.

The core principles explored and observed by Dr. Kumar and Professor Tripathi are as follows –

  1. Founder’s philosophy and core values
  2. Leadership with trust
  3. Community as the key purpose of the enterprise
  4. Senior leaders as mentors and role-models
  5. Ethical code of conduct
  6. Employee-focus
  7. Tacit alignment with Indian cultural values

On being asked what Dr Kumar enjoyed the most about writing this paper, Dr. Abhishek said that visiting the roots of TATA Group was a very satisfying experience as he was born and brought up in Jamshedpur. Dr. Abhishek explains that in some sense it was an exploration of his own identity. He loved conceptualising the seven identified humanistic values in terms of terminal and instrumental values and presenting them in an elegant theoretical hypothesis.

Webinar: Conscious Fashion: Materials of the Future

Rahul Kumar Sahu, B.Des. | Vinay Srivastava, B.des
Image Courtesy: Luca Laurence from Unsplash

We consume textiles, as necessities and fashion, undermining the effect it may have on the environment. We live in an economic system widely known as 'The Materials Economy'. This revenue-driven system, which extracts raw materials and processes them into usable materials, is environmentally, socially and culturally unsustainable. To this end, AnantU’s Fashion and Textile Design [Sustainable Innovation] department, on the 14th of October, organised a webinar on ‘Conscious Fashion: Materials of the Future’ to enlighten future designers and fashion enthusiasts on the harmful effects of fashion and how to make the industry more sustainable.

Organised as part of the 'Sustainable Soft Material', offered as minor by the department, the webinar had sustainability champions Marina Chahboune, Founder of Closed Loop Fashion, Zuzana Gombosova, A biochemist and Founder of Malai Biomaterials and Shikha Shah, Founder of Ahmedabad based AltMat as the panellists. Through the webinar, the panellists shared their experiences and vision for the future by working towards replacing the 'Materials economy' through green innovations that are environmentally, socially and culturally viable.

The webinar focussed on the fashion and textile industry, which is one of the most polluting industries in the world, and how it should be revamped to make it more eco-friendly and sustainable. It focussed on changes to be made in the production and consumption of textiles that are in line with the surrounding and the environment. It questioned the products that are utilised and how it can be made better to save the environment and its future.

The conversation brought a lot of value to the table as the panellists walked the attendees through everything ranging from the effects of cotton and hemp, their start-up journey, what they believe the world deserves and everything that the students are currently being educated on. The webinar ended with a Question and Answer session that shed light on the practical, consistent efforts being put by conscious experts, such as our speakers, to safeguard the planet.

Dr Amareswar Galla Serves as Keynote Speaker at an International Conference

Anuvacha Team

Dr Amareswar Galla, Director, ICICL, AnantU, served as a keynote speaker and Co-chair of the Conference, elected by the Vietnam Academy of Social Sciences, at the international conference on 'Innovative Methods of Organising Exhibition: Lessons for Vietnam'.

In his keynote speech, 'Exhibiting Global South: Vietnam and Beyond'., Professor Galla answered questions such as what design thinking for exhibitions as critical mediators for communicating heritage values? Is design thinking truly interdisciplinary? If so, how could it become useful in future museum development and transformations in Vietnam and India? How does it all translate during the Pandemic and afterwards?

Professor Galla also reflected on his experiences gathered while he was working on the development of the museum in Hanoi, a major demonstration project in Southeast Asia, for over a decade where he trained its staff and partners from 2000. His speech highlighted how Indians are chasing western museums, because of colonial hangovers, without the capacity or capability to ape them. He also suggests that there is more to learn from Asian countries such as Vietnam for Indian museums and exhibit designers.

Organised by Vietnam Museum of Ethnology-VME-, Hanoi, Vietnam, the conference served as a scientific forum aiming at exchanging and updating the concepts, methods and new trends in exhibition work of museums among national and international experts in the direction of modern approaches and international integration. The conference focussed on three major themes: Approaches in developing exhibitions; new trends in organising exhibitions in Vietnam and the world; and lessons learnt for the Vietnam Museum of Ethnology.

Dr Dang Xuan Thanh, Director, Vietnam Museum of Ethnology, thanked Professor Galla for sharing his experience and knowledge which was of immense help in understanding the role of museums and how museums would help people connect with the world better.

Featured Projects

3D printed Robotic Arm Demonstrator

A robotic arm, demonstrator model, was made for the benefit of our students to understand the basic working of a Robotic arm. It is a type of mechanical arm, usually programmable, with similar functions to a human arm. The arm may be the total of the mechanism or may be part of a more complex robot. This model can demonstrate how a Robotic arm is operated and how the commands are sent to it from a programmable hardware

The model was taken from the creative common type of resource as a downloadable STL assembly data. This was then 3D printed in our in-house 3D printer. Additionally, the servomotors and the basic power and intelligence was provided from our Electronics Workbench. The robot operated by programmes running on our Arduino Uno boards, that send motion commands to the arms servo motors. These then execute the motions and demonstrate the inner working of a typical Robotic Arm. The project was made using Poly Lactic Acid and ABS filaments, the arms are sturdy and have great strength.

The project was an initiative of the MakerSpace team, which employed technological subjects like automation and technical design.

Smart Chair

The MakerSpace team designed a prototype of a folding chair which can be turned into a smart chair. This prototype continuously detects the position of the users back in reference to the backrest of the chair. The moment the distance falls into a range indicating slouching posture, the buzzer immediately notifies the user, so s/he can avoid slouching posture. The project was made using a bamboo chair with added intelligence of an ultrasonic range finder and a programmable Arduino board. The chair can solve medical problems of the spine, and the circuit can also be used in the automobile car seat so that the driver can easily control its movements.

Tensor Table

The Makerspace team created the tensor table also known as the levitation table to demonstrate how the table surface stays put despite the absence of any solid connection between the base and the top. Levitation is the process by which an object is held aloft, without mechanical support, in a stable position. Levitation is accomplished by providing an upward that counteracts the pull of gravity, plus a smaller stabilizing force that pushes the object toward a home position whenever it is a small distance away from that home position.

1. A Cat to be Officially Ordained Stationmaster!

There’s a cat in Japan, named Yontama, who is the latest in a line of officially ordained stationmasters of the Wakayama Electric railway. The first, Tama, though deceased, holds the positions of ‘Honorary Eternal Stationmaster’ and spirit goddess ‘Tama Daimyōjin’ (たま大明神).

This bizarre but cute situation is also the very definition of ‘Nekonomics’ in practice, a term coined by the Japanese that refers to how cats can have an influence on the economy. In this case, Tama and his successors single-handedly saved the Kishigawa railway line in Japan’s Wakayama prefecture from being shut down.

2. Brain Abnormality! What’s that?

A man of French nationality, who despite having most of his brain eroded, lives a relatively normal, healthy life as a civil servant married with two children.

The majority of his brain was compressed into a thin layer of tissue lining his skull as a result of fluid build-up from a condition called ‘hydrocephalus’.

Amazingly, despite his minimal remaining brain tissue, the man wasn't mentally disabled - he had a low IQ of 75, but was working as a civil servant and had a normal family.

Though it merely seems like a miracle, it has wider implications, especially in understanding ‘human consciousness’ and how we as humans perceive it.

Even though this man’s remaining brain was tiny, the leftover neurons were still able to generate a theory about themselves, which means the man remained conscious of his actions.

This renewed understanding of the capabilities of the human brain (which are still largely mysterious to us) can help in understanding and reversing illnesses that result in neurodegeneration.

In the above image, the black region is all fluid, the thin grey matter lining the skull is all that is remaining of the man’s brain - 10% of what is present in normal human beings.

Nameless Beagle

Rahul Kumar Sahu, B.Des.

The beagle walked down the streets of Delhi looking for scraps of garbage in his search for food. The busy roads of the city lost the crowd that used to walk upon it once. It felt as if the beings on the streets were an outcast, whose existence was ignored in the face of the pandemic.

He walked on the street aimlessly. Survival was something that had become intrinsic to his behaviour. With each passing day, he was leaving behind his old self and gaining his wild feral nature that his ancestors had abandoned. He even forgot the name given to him by his old master.

When the whole thing began, countless news popped on the smartphone of the Verma family’s patriarch. One of the news had the following headline: “Studies show that dogs spread Coronavirus”

The news had no scientific backing, but the patriarch had a responsibility to keep his family safe. After careful consideration, he chose to let the dog out of the house. The dog joyfully went out, as he expected his master to join him for a walk. Instead, he heard the doors shut.

The family tried to convince him to let the beagle in, but he had to take the hard call.

The beagle kept barking for quite a while.

The patriarch felt the pain of the dog, but with the irritation that each successive bark brought to the ambience of the place, that pain mutated into anger.

He opened the door. The dog was happy to see his master, but the master was not. He picked up a stone and threw at the dog. He screamed at him, as the responsibility of keeping his family safe weighed on him.

Scared, the beagle ran away from the place he knew as home.

For the first few days, he followed all that remotely smelled like food but everywhere the food was in the same condition: In a dumpster with a repulsive stench.

Till that point, he searched as hard as he could, but now the desperation of his rumbling stomach compelled him to take a bite of the leftover meat on chicken bones.

He ate a piece of meat.

It was as repulsive as it smelled, but the food in his mouth brought the desire of his hungry belly to the forefront. He didn’t want to be hungry; he had to swallow it down.

As the food reached down to his belly, it was a strange satisfaction.

With time, he adapted and started scavenging for food in all sorts of places. However, in the growing days of lockdown, the beagle found the number of his food source shrinking. He started expanding the area of his food search. During his hunt, he came across a couple of street dogs he had met for the first time. The beagle had seen dogs, but these gave a vibe of uncivilized.

One of the big dogs came forward. Grim was imprinted on his face. He gave out a series of loud barks, directed towards the beagle. There was no intent of communication in those barks.

They were pure malice.

A signal of retaliation for the one who encroached on their territory.
