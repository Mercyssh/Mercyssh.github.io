The Paradox of Vacant Buildings in India

Previous Next
Dhwanee Shah, B.Des

AnantU e-launched the first city series of the investigative report on ‘The Paradox of Vacant Buildings in India’, on 27th August, 2020. The ‘paradox’ in this context refers to the fact that while a large section of the population does not have access to decent housing in urban areas in India, there is a wide stock of vacant buildings in the same areas.

The report examines significant determining factors of vacant houses in Ahmedabad and suggests long-term measures to make housing available for all. It is an extension of the round table discussion on the Paradox of Vacant Houses in India, which was held on 10th May 2019 at the Anant Centre for Sustainability.

AnantU invited a panel of India’s top experts on urban housing in India for the launch of the event. The panellists comprised Anil Antony (National Convenor & Parliamentarian with Innovators of India), David Smith (Founder and CEO of the Affordable Housing Institute in Boston, USA), Dr. Anunaya Chaubey (Provost, Anant National University), Dhaval Monani (Director, Affordable Housing, Anant Centre for Sustainability), and Dr Zeeshan (Assistant Professor and Senior Researcher, Anant Centre for Sustainability). Miniya Chatterjee (Director, Anant Centre for Sustainability & CEO, Sustain Labs, Paris) was the moderator of the event.

The panellists discussed how affordable housing units in India could be increased by introducing incentives, and through the effective use of vacant houses during disaster relief measures such as the COVID-19 pandemic. AnantU has transformed various vacant buildings across the country into COVID-19 recovery facilities and has set an example on how to use vacant houses resourcefully. The University gained media coverage for its various initiatives on vacant housing and COVID-19 relief activities in the recent past.

AnantU alumnus on winning COA’s International Essay Writing Competition

Previous Next
Saipriya Yellayi, B.Des.

Vir Shah, B.Arch. alumnus won the second prize in the International Essay Writing Competition organised by the Council of Architecture. Themed on 'Role of Architecture in Nation Building', Vir's essay 'UNTITLED', highlighted the importance of people participating in the process of nation-building alongside architecture and design. Keep reading as he divulges more details about the essay, the thought process behind it, his aims and aspirations, and his rendezvous with architecture.

Q. What inspired you to pursue architecture?

A question I’d never have an answer for and I suppose I like it that way. It keeps my drive alive for understanding the discipline. However, I chose architecture at a later stage in my life. Until then, I had a cursory understanding of the field. Looking back, I feel that the physical surroundings I was brought up in played a vital role subconsciously, in my decision to follow this path and imbibed a liking for the art and craft. In the five years of understanding and learning architecture, I was introduced to a whole new dimension and explored various perspectives that helped me in perceiving architecture, not as a profession but rather an experience and a medium. To me, architecture is more about humanising and sensitising spaces and experiences. It is not just about designing shelters but thinking through one’s minds, conditions and milieu in which one survives.

Q. What ideas did you want to convey through your essay?

I attempted to address the questions before asserting what architecture is and what role does it play into the process of nation-building, i.e. “Is architecture used at its full potential as a tool to build the nation with sensitive efficiency? Or, is there a need for widening the range of planes where it operates?”. I have attempted to answer these questions through my essay alongside identifying some broader domains where architecture has a significant role to play.

1. For sincere participation of the people in nation-building, architecture must invoke the ancestral roots and pull out cultural threads from the surviving specimens of ancient times.

2. Temporal and transient articulations of space are often dismissed as hindrances to the protection of an ‘image’ to our global contemporaries. However, architecture’s dissociation with such informal mechanisms of the city results in critical, hazardous and volatile living conditions.

3. Architectural practices in cities and towns present the profession as a highly urban phenomenon detached from the slow morphing of Indian villages. Sustainable built spaces must support participatory schemes that ensure economic viability in the continuation of farming practices and vernacular crafts.

4. Cities allow us to shed the baggage of labels imposed upon by society and assume individualistic identities. Cities are conglomerations where the country’s talent accumulates and builds expertise. Hence, cities can be considered the building blocks of a nation. The architects should design cities in a way to accommodate these pluralists identities, changing lifestyle patterns and social needs.

5. New cities are like laboratories for experimentation in architecture. They serve as the ground to test theories and concepts that best suit the context. For a nation to progress, these experiments are crucial and as non-negotiable as those in the fields of medicine, agriculture and technology.

To be honest, I never thought of using the platform as a medium to share my ideas on this scale. I feel fortunate and grateful for getting an opportunity to put forth my thoughts.

Q. Which elements from India’s past architecture should be implemented for a better future?

The inclusive, visionary and extrovert nature of India’s architecture has been a major driving force and helped put India in the forefront. Significant buildings have showcased India’s talent and have become symbols of innovation and progress. Indian architecture is closely associated with the complex web of social, political and economic forces which shaped the vision of the then independent India and had put out a word of comfort among the people, reminding them of their freedom and rights. Cultural inferences, sensitivity, region and vision were significant factors of architectural marvels of that era.

Q. Any piece of wisdom that would help future architects make the nation better?

It’s a quest and more importantly about carving possibilities for the future without disrupting links with the past, to quote my essay.

I am unsure how India’s growing architecture will turn out to be, but it is important to understand that every era has had its nature, character and link. However, we also need to accept the evolving nature of the discipline and that, through spatial morphologies, we will be able to develop perceptions relative to our immediate surroundings. Keeping this in mind and acting accordingly will help us shape the future of architecture.

Advaita Jairam, B.Des.

Good and healthy lifestyle choices help in improving mood, reducing stress, keeping self active and preventing mental illness. To this end, BLOOM, AnantU’s Centre for Wellbeing, recently hosted webinars that aimed at improving the daily lifestyle of AnantU students. These are especially helpful in times when online classes have reduced the scope of human interaction and pose challenges for students who are new to the system. These webinars guided us on how to deal with and adapt to the ‘new normal’ and gave a safe space to freely discuss our worries and receive advice.

How to Improve Focus

The centre hosted a webinar on ‘How to Improve Focus’ - an issue that is quite common among students, especially since everyone is at home and distractions come easy in the form of entertainment, thus making assignments a difficult feat.

Jinal Doshi, the presenter, had a few tips for the students. For example, the use of a timer instead of a clock, enabling you to know how much time is remaining rather than how much is over - which is much more effective and motivating! Another thing is that while rewarding yourself is good, it is also necessary for you to scold yourself when necessary so you can get work done. Managing and keeping track of your priorities is also important, and according to her, it is advisable to keep aside 15-20 minutes free in your daily schedule for emergencies. This way, your schedule can be flexible in the case of unforeseen circumstances. Also, when your close friends or family want your time, and you are torn over choosing between them and your work - you can use the aforementioned free time or, if you can, postpone it to your downtime.

In addition to these, several other tips are in the header image, use them to make the most of your time. Better still, attend the next Bloom webinar and get advice on your problems in person!

Better Sleeping Tips

A short video around the topic ‘Better Sleeping Tips’ was also released recently - the simple and succinct video details out a few steps to sleep better. Sleep well and maintaining a steady circadian rhythm has always been a major problem for a lot of people, especially students.

In fact, irregular sleep schedules have become synonymous with the idea of ‘college life’. However, this doesn’t change the fact that sleep is an important part of our daily life and plays a major role in productivity levels throughout the day. So, to work efficiently, we must sleep better and the video lists out 7 tips for the same.

According to the video, we must stop using electronic gadgets at least an hour before bedtime. We must avoid drinking tea, coffee, excess water, or alcohol before going to sleep, and not eat a heavy meal for dinner. Smoking, too, is a big no-no at bedtime. Apart from these, it is also recommended that we avoid intellectually stimulating activities before bedtime. Lastly, in order to have a good, uninterrupted rest at night, we should avoid taking daytime naps.

Most of these are truths that we students commonly ignore, but just think about it this way - that assignment that you’ve been struggling to finish? How about you sleep on it and try again the next day? A well-rested mind is more productive than a sleep-deprived one - so you just might get a great idea if only you give your mind time to rest and rejuvenate.

Suicide Awareness

Suicide is the leading cause of death among Indian youth. What makes this worse is that suicide is also the most avoidable form of death in the world.

Sadly, this is mainly due to the stigma of suicide as a topic due to which people avoid talking about it all-together - hence preventing the essential discussions on the futility of suicide, and another life lost in vain.

Hence, in honour of World Suicide Prevention Day (September 10), BLOOM and YourDOST, AnantU’s partner for student counselling, have come together to launch the #YouAreNotAlone Initiative - a collaborative approach to educating ourselves as a community, in order to help those who are struggling in their lives and make sure that their pleas are heard and addressed - and therefore, saving a life.

As a part of this initiative, many resources will be provided to the students to help them become more aware and so they can learn how to help those around them and lend them a resuscitating helping-hand. Certainly, there’s nothing better than saving someone’s life and helping them on their journey to self-recovery!

Heritage Matters 8 and 9

Ritu Vora, B.Arch.

Launched on the occasion of World Heritage Day, the ‘Heritage Matters’ webinar series brings forth various aspects of heritage and suggests ways to preserve this legacy. Till date, AnanatU’s International Center for Inclusive Cultural Leadership (ICICL) has conducted seven live and interactive webinars which delved on topics such as the role of museums in the post-pandemic world; how building strong relations across geographic and cultural boundaries will help the community combat crisis such as COVID-19 pandemic and climate change; the need of dynamic documentation of the crisis around us among others. These webinars aim at initiating conversations to identify root problems, ideate solutions and spread awareness about the issues that affect the masses together.

In continuation of the series, ICICL conducted the 8th webinar on ‘Safeguarding Intangible Cultural Heritage as part of the two-day virtual event, ‘Safeguarding the Intangible Cultural Heritage and Diverse Cultural Traditions of India’, held on 9th-10th August 2020.

Organised on the occasion of the International Day of the World’s Indigenous Peoples, the webinar took the attendees on a virtual journey to Arna Jharna: The Thar Desert Museum at Jodhpur in Rajasthan, who hosted the blended webinars and festivals. The festival comprised performances and exhibitions of artworks from the Langa, Kamad, Kalbelia, and Mangariyar communities and celebrated their work through pre-recorded sessions.

The celebration started with a performance by children of the ‘Langa’ community, followed by thinker and cultural activist, Padma Shri G.N. Devy’s speech. Kulbhushan Kothari, Chairman of Lok Samvad Sansthan and former Director UNICEF, New York also spoke on the occasion.

The webinars, hosted as part of the festival, aimed at raising awareness for preservation, promotion, and addressing the needs and concerns of Indigenous people. The panellists for the webinars comprised scholars, professors and directors, who spoke on the importance of the changing environment, current pandemic and issues and measures concerning the safeguarding of our cultural heritage. Dr Amareswar Galla, Professor of Inclusive Cultural Leadership & Director, International Centre for Inclusive Cultural Leadership (ICICL) at AnantU, Director of International Institute for the Inclusive Museum and a UNESCO Heritage expert, delivered the keynote speech at the event.

The two-day virtual fest ended with a theatrical theatre dance and drama of Kerala by Indira Gandhi National Centre for the Arts for UNESCO called ‘Mudiyettu’ followed by ‘Kalbelia documentary’ and a museum curatorial tour.

Webinar 9 titled ‘Valuing Modern Heritage’, held on 22nd August 2020, focussed on the lesser represented and largely unprotected architecture heritage of early 20th century India. The panellists for the webinar were Eric Falt, Director, UNESCO South Asia Office; Amita Baig, India representative for the World Monuments Fund; Dr. Shikha Jain, Past Advisor and multiple grantee for the Keeping It Modern initiative of the Getty Foundation; and Nishita Kedia, AnantU Faculty. It presented perspectives on how Modern Heritage of India is yet to be recognised for its true value and is given the status of statutory protection. It discussed how international organizations like UNESCO, ICOMOS, WMF, Getty Foundation and others are advocating for the recognition of Modern Heritage in India. Further, it identified the role of government bodies, the status of protection and community awareness that determines the fate of modern heritage in future.

Professor Nishita Kedia, AnantU faculty and one of the panellists, led these discussions and addressed questions such as, What is the nature and quantum of Modern Heritage in India? How is it different from other parts of the world? Who owns and who values this heritage? What is the representation of Modern Heritage on the World Heritage List of India? What is the protection status of Modern 20th-century heritage considering the fact that ASI Act only recognises heritage structures more than 100 years old? Does the general public associate with Modern Heritage? The answers to all these questions enlightened the attendees to think forward about the terms related to our heritage.

The Dancing House, Prague, Czech Republic

Previous Next
Dhwanee Shah, B.Des. | Photographs: Self Clicked

In 2019, in a pre-COVID world, as I was blissfully walking through the colourful, and often busy streets of Prague dominated by Baroque, Gothic and Art Nouveau buildings, I noticed a structure which stood out from its neighbours. Having seen this building in photos and reading about it in the books, it was surreal when I chanced upon it by coincidence. It felt like staring at the face of a celebrity, the only difference being that my celebrity was this beautiful building which looked like it was dancing.

Also known as the Ginger and Fred, after the legendary dance duo Ginger Rogers and Fred Astair, the Dancing House was designed by Croatian-Czech architect Vlado Milunić in co-operation with the renowned Canadian-American architect Frank Gehry. The building was designed in 1992 and completed in 1996.

The building was considered controversial at the time because, in the opinion of some, it was non-traditional and did not blend well with its surroundings that consisted majorly of Baroque and Art Nouveau buildings. Set on a property of great historical significance, its site was the location of a house which was destroyed during the bombing of Prague by the United States in 1945. From their first meeting in 1992 in Geneva, Gehry and Milunić began to elaborate Milunić’s original idea of a building consisting of two parts, static and dynamic (“yin and yang”), which were to symbolize the political transition of Czechoslovakia (Czechia) from a communist regime to parliamentary democracy.

The style is known as deconstructivist architecture due to its unusual shape. The “dancing” shape is supported by 99 concrete panels, each a different shape and dimension. On the top of the building is a large twisted structure of metal nicknamed Medusa. The building harbours nine floors, with two floors underground.

The Dancing House has two central bodies. The first is a tower of glass that is close to half its height and is supported by curved pillars, and the second which runs parallel to the River Vltava is characterized by the mouldings that follow a wavy motion. Additionally, the winding mouldings on the facade make it a more confusing perspective, diminishing the contrast with the buildings that surround it.

The general profile of the building is now featured on a gold 2,000 Czech koruna coin issued by the Czech National Bank. The coin completes a series called the “Ten Centuries of Architecture”.

In present times, the building serves as a newly opened luxury hotel, offering 21 luxurious rooms with a view of Prague Castle, a café and a rooftop restaurant, with a beautiful panoramic view of the city. The building is now seen as a work of art, adding value to the cityscape of Prague.

Benefits of Journaling

Deep Tak, B.Des.

Journaling… sounds boring? Give it a whirl and you will be amazed with what you find. It is the simple act of thinking about your life and writing about it. Despite the benefits, many people find it boring. Journaling has played a key role in some of the greatest people in history. It is a good hobby to pick up during this time as we have limited time due to assignments and doing something productive takes a lot of time, unlike; journaling just requires you to write only a few lines everyday. Everyone can benefit from writing their thoughts on paper. Here are some points which will help you get motivated.

  • Journaling provides us the opportunity to learn from our past experiences.

“Found the significance to lie where I never saw it at the time” - Virginia Woolf

  • Journaling helps in brain function and memory. People who pick up this habit can often define how they were feeling when they were much younger.
  • Journaling can also be a good motivator. When you write about something, you can find yourself on a much clearer path and making it easier to document your self progress.

Despite all the benefits of journaling, there is just one problem; people don't usually stick with the idea of it. While it may sound good in theory, it's a hard habit to pick up. Take it up as a challenge and dare yourself to do journalling. Make to-do lists for everyday and experience the difference it will make in your life!

Ayush Deshpande, B.Des.

For any modern civilisation, communication is very important. Without the ability to talk to each other, and exchange ideas and information, we would be living either in complete chaos or isolation.

Even though most of us have the tools that we need to communicate effectively and efficiently, many of us don’t. We keep our thoughts to ourselves, avoid awkward situations and conflicts and even let things build within ourselves until they become overwhelming. All of this to avoid openly expressing and letting our innermost thoughts out.

We humans are funny!

Animation is a very powerful communication tool. It provides us with a medium to either exaggerate or scale down the images to better focus on the story and its message in a way that communicates its meaning effectively. Animation can inform, narrate, explain or even instruct. It has a very high potential to build up empathy and even an emotional connection with the viewer.
