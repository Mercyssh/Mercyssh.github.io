DN HALL - An Architectural Marvel of Vadodara

Previous Next
Radhika Gupta, B.Arch (1st-year)

D.N Hall also known as Dadabhai Navroji Hall is the pride of The Architecture Department of Maharaja Sayajirao University (MSU). Located in the heart of the main campus of MSU, it is an Architectural marvel designed in 1915. It was originally designed as a guest house for British Officials and then later as a boys hostel till the early 1990s.

It was later converted into the administrative wing for the Department of Architecture and a new building was designed at its rear end for the Academic Section.

Looking at the campus from a philosophical point of view, one discovers the subtle expressions and design intentions that make an individual connect with it on a deeper level, rather than considering it as a mere architectural piece.

As Christian Wolff has rightly quoted “Architecture is a science for constructing a building so that it is in complete correspondence with architect’s intention”, The intention was to create a structure which is a sculpture in itself with all the functional aspects and forms the base of inspiration for the students.

The building in itself looks perfect and raw in its appearance. But it is the exposed brick and concrete walls dotted with murals and sculptures that bring about a strong sense of belonging. The walls of the campus are like a canvas for its students, where they are free to paint their expressions. These walls adorn the murals and art pieces of its students like a queen adorns her crown. Proudly flaunting her beauty to her spectators.

Every nook and corner of the campus holds blissful memories of different generations of Architects. Each batch of students has carved out space for themselves, their very first design as an undergraduate.

‘THE POND’ – which is the fruit of a long night of labor. Now it’s the most happening space which has witnessed numerous students being thrown inside as a long-standing Birthday Tradition!

‘THE OPEN AMPHI’ – Which gave the dean a heart attack! It came out of nowhere one casual Monday morning. But now hosts several casual discussions during classes and lunch breaks.

And not to forget, the beloved furry friend of all the students and teachers of the campus – MUSKY. The four-legged dog holds the record of attending a maximum number of lectures.

Looking from the perspective of the well-renowned philosopher and designer Ludwig Wittgenstein, the building is a juxtaposition of cubes and cuboids placed upon each other. Voids are created to generate a form. It is free from all kinds of details and articulation and beautiful in its simplistic form. The aesthetics are articulated through the careful proportions of the rooms and spaces.

The grand double-height terraces, play in the ceiling heights and a careful arrangement taking care of the symmetry, geometry, and harmony of the spaces create a sense of interactiveness between the space and its users.

But most importantly, this campus is a gift. It is passed down from one generation of Architects to another, with a promise that the campus would always be proud of them. The campus was designed by ‘The Kalabhavan Association of Architects’ and is a product of their love for their college and juniors. And so even though a passer-by or outsider may consider this campus an institutional building, to its students it would also feel like a home away from home.

Dr Subhalaxmi Mohapatra Wins the Case Award for her Research Paper on Beeja

Previous Next
Advaita Jairam, B.Des

Dr Subhalaxmi Mohapatra, Associate Professor, recently received the second-best Case Award at the 10th International Conference on Management Cases – 2020 for her research paper titled, “Beeja: Sprout into Bloom or Wither into Doom”.

Organised by BIMTECH Noida in collaboration with the University of Eastern Finland and the University of South Florida, Dr Mohapatra co-authored the paper with Dr Subhadip Roy, Associate Professor, IIMA.

The case study documented the dilemma of the founders of Beeja - a social entrepreneurship venture that seeks to create alternate livelihood opportunities for the crafts community.

“The present case study is about Beeja, an organisation formed in 2005 as UCMA design studio to create sustainable communities among rural farmers by providing them training and assistance, which could provide alternative livelihood options to rural farmers", said Dr Mohapatra.

"The primary-data based case illustrates the problem of the founders' dilemma. Besides, It highlights the role of organisational structures in the success and continuance of a firm. The case brings out various challenges faced by social entrepreneurs. The paper serves as an example of the Agency theory as it centres around the principal-agent problem and links to theories of social entrepreneurship and ethics", she added.

Professor Ashima Banker Wins Incubation Support for Research

Previous Next
Advaita Jairam, B.Des

Professor Ashima Banker and her team won incubation support for her research on 'Prefabricated Prefinished Volumetric Construction’ for ASHA-India scheme by MoHUA.

Professor Banker presented her project in the 'Preprototype Potential Technology' category as a part of the ‘Global Housing Technology Challenge – India’, a platform to mainstream innovative and sustainable construction technologies in the affordable housing sector. The Ministry of Housing and Urban Affairs (MoHUA), Government of India, launched the challenge in February 2019.

The project will receive technical and financial support for future absorption in the construction sector, for product development, mentoring and monitoring, prototyping, company incorporation, patent guidance and business model assistance, through incubation and acceleration at premier technical institutes (4 IITs identified) and BMTPC in collaboration with selected Knowledge Partners (KPs).

Additionally, six winning entries after the incubation, will assist in constructing 1000 houses at each of the identified six sites across India. These affordable housing projects are under the Pradhan Mantri Awas Yojana (PMAY).

“In February 2019, I received an invitation from World Research Institute (WRI), India (knowledge partner to MoHUA), to attend Expo-cum-Conference on 2-3 March 2019 at Vigyan Bhavan, New Delhi, and do a poster presentation for the category 'Potential Future Technologies' for the housing sector”, said Professor Banker.

After the approval from Dr Pramath Raj Sinha, the team submitted their entry in the form of a poster. The poster was selected among the top eight entries from 80+ entries and was presented to the honorary Prime Minister Narendra Modi, during the event held on 2-3 March 2019 in Delhi.

“Our entry got shortlisted in June 2019 and we were invited to make the final pitch, on 3rd July 2019 in Delhi, to the eminent panel (comprising representatives from IIT Chennai, IIT Mumbai, BMTPC, HUDCO and MoHUA). We were amongst the 20 odd entries shortlisted”, Professor Banker added.

The team received a formal intimation from MoHUA stating their win. The idea will be incubated in IIT Roorkee for technical and financial support for future absorption. The winners were formally announced during the ASHA-India awards online event on 1st Jan 2021.

“We hope that winning this challenge will prove to be a great opportunity for us and our students for the learning experience we gained from it during the product development and prototyping phase”, said Professor Ashima Banker.

Smart Jacket for Social Distancing

Previous Next
Deep Tak, B.Des

The pandemic has inspired everyone to discover and learn new things. While we continue to develop and progress with our lives adjusting to the current lack of social interaction, a student decided to do something about this drastic change.

Nakul Sharma, currently pursuing his post graduation in Integrated Product Design from AnantU, inspired by his personal experiences and creative learning in the field of developing softwares and design came up with a solution, a smart social distancing jacket.

Nakul along with Milind Chitale (Associate Professor, AnantU), Parth Atulkumar Shah (Assistant Professor, AnantU) and Nikita Bavishi Shah (Assistant Professor, Sal College of Engineering) decided to progress with his idea and started working towards his design process.

A smart jacket equipped with an Arduino + 3 ultrasonic distance sensors, a small LCD display, battery and buzzers can be the next step towards smart clothing. Combining both technology and fashion, the jacket alerts its user about the distance of people around him/her. If anyone crosses within a certain radius of the jacket, a coded program in arduino triggers the buzzers and alerts the user as well as the people around him/her.

While the idea sounds simple, Nakul explains its complexities. The design process is complex as it raises issues like the weight of the jacket, how people will react to it and how comfortable it will be. Observing and perfecting such issues is the key to a good product.

Doing extensive user research both physically and online, Nakul explains that ideas of somewhat similar comparison exist, it only makes a difference if you work on them instead of just perfecting them in theory.

While the idea is still in the mill and Nakul continues to work on the prototype, he says that hands-on learning besides ideation is equally important for complete understanding, gaining confidence and making minor adjustments to the innovation which can not be observed in theory. One should not be demotivated because an idea already exists, in a world of 7.8 billion people there will always be ideas already thought upon but only a very few actually worked upon them.

Nakul believes that the innovation will open a lot of scope about smart wear and can help not just in social distancing but also a plethora of other things as well.

AnantU students Cheshta Jain, Jaival Mehta, Jyot Mankad, Radhika Patel, and Zeal Bagadia recently participated in an online workshop, ‘Reading Master Practices’, an online programme, organised by the Institute of Indian Interior Designers (IIID), Ahmedabad Regional Chapter. M.Des. (UD) Students’ Entry Shortlisted in Placemaking competition

Previous Next
Sudarshan Singh, B.Des

Gaurav Sonkavde, Mruga Raut and Sahil Fadia, students of Master of Design in Urban Design, along with students from Pratt Institute, participated in the Placemaking Competition and were shortlisted in the top 20, which received more than 100 entries. Organised by Trust for Regeneration of Indian Settlements (TRIS India) and Multilogue Collective (MLC), the competition required them to reimagine a public space to enhance socialisation, community engagement, inclusion and equity of that place.

The public place chosen for a real-life experience was Chitranjan Park in Delhi. It represents a mini-Bengal in itself, after being built in the name of Indian activist Chitranjan Das. The place is a blend of Bengali culture and modernity. While re-designing the space, the students emphasised socio-cultural interactions, inclusion and barrier-free movement across the park.

The project focussed on planned parking spaces through planters for people to avoid the crowd. Provisions were made for all categories including a ramp for the disabled, to a proper sitting and whiling space for the fast-food eaters. In contrast to the current unorganised parking space at the entrance, the newly designed space has trees for shading the parked vehicles. Comfort has been taken care of with multiple sitting spaces all around the park, utilising the staircase as a sitting space as well. Vintage elements from Bengal culture were used, such as the green windows, yellow ambassador car, a rectangular pattern umbrella in addition to the reminiscence of 1970’s Satyajit Ray films. An activity area has been designed for children to play board games, hopscotch et al.

The students designed the project with an agenda of helping the community and upraising the Bengali culture. The competition helped students work in collaboration and learn from peers, along with international exposure. It allowed them to work on a real-life project which exposed them to critical urban design processes ranging from community engagement, stakeholder consultation, placemaking to street design.

B.Arch Students Participate for MSL and GSEN Trophy

Previous Next
Rahul Kumar Sahu, B.Des

B.Arch students participated in two trophies: MSL Trophy and GSEN Trophy. Themed on Rudiments, preparation for each of the competitions were different. The works produced by students highlighted their technical, analytical, and teamwork skills.

Second-year B.Arch students with guidance from their seniors and faculty participated in the Mohammad Shaheer Landscape(MSL) Trophy. The project required them to explore the balance between art, science, and nature through a landscape intervention.

The students chose Anant National University as their site and worked around four areas on the campus. They analysed the soil profile and climatology, amongst other things to get an idea of the site. To get a thorough understanding of the use case and the users, they mapped out activities, demographics, user groups, and the site's evolution with time. With data collected around the space, they started ideating.

For the space near the Eiffel tower, a scattered illusion of the institution’s tagline was designed to emphasise the University’s ethos. Ideas for introducing the gurukul culture were implemented by having stone seating under the shady trees. Spaces were converted for appreciation and pathways for a moment of tranquillity.

The G-Sen trophy was centred on urban design with agricultural productivity and enhancement of biodiversity for resilient landscapes. The 12-member group chose the site of the area near the NID Bridge, where they worked on the integration of the farming landscape in an urban context. It was about compensating nature for things that we took away from her. They did their site survey, analysis, and documentation which helped them design the interventions.

The ideas had microscopic elements such as integrating roof gardening, backyard farming, hydroponics, and pot farming. The sack method of growing vegetables for societies in slums and an eco-corridor was designed for the animals. Everything tied into the bigger picture of a social structure that had a barter system to exchange foods and use the wastes as biodiesel for vehicles. A social program was planned with different modules that would be overseen by an administration built for ensuring the smooth working of this system.

The competition helped participants learn many things for the first time through a hands-on approach. However, amidst this pandemic, the projects proved to be an enriching experience as it enabled everyone to socialise and build something great.

Student Aditi Bhalekar’s Scrub Design Worn by Najambaug Medical Staff

Previous Next
Saipriya Yellayi, B.Des.

The pandemic has forced everyone to ponder over the regular course of life and break free of the mediocrity through creative yet practical design innovations. Anant National University, along with its staff and students, has embarked on the journey where pandemic, instead of a hindrance, has proved to be an opportunity to create unique design solutions for existing and new problems.

Anant Centre for Sustainability has championed the cause of COVID-19 related innovations such as setting up COVID-19 recovery centres, corrugated cardboard beds and related furniture, Anant Mobile COVID-19 Testing and Oxygen Auto-rickshaw, among others. In a recent initiative, the Centre had launched a competition where they asked the AnantU students to design cost-effective scrubs for healthcare professionals.

Aditi Bhalekar, a foundation year student, won the contest by designing an easy to wear, cost-effective scrub with storage space for personal belongings. Aditi designed high quality-acceptable cotton-mix jumpsuit tailored for the purpose of holding personal possessions and medical equipment, can be easily taken off and comfortable to use for long periods. The jumpsuit consisted of multiple pockets, front zipper, medium-length sleeves and was priced at only Rs 280, which is Rs 60 less than the existing scrubs. The minute detail of printing ‘COVID Warrior’ at the back of the scrub served as a twinge of positivity and motivation for those keeping their lives in danger to save patients.

Used by the medical staff at Anant COVID–19 recovery facility in Najambaug, Mumbai, the scrubs have received a positive response from over 50 nurses and doctors. AnantU is now planning to provide these scrubs in existing as well as upcoming COVID-19 Recovery centres. “I am glad the design found acceptance by its end users,” said Aditi. “This whole process has been a huge learning curve for me.”

Workshop: Undivided Identities: Unknown Stories of the Partition

Previous Next
Advaita Jairam, B.Des

Recently, Garima Sharma, Advaita Jairam, Laddhi Mehta, Namrata Swargari, Pari Nirmal, Ashlesha Anchan, Janhavi Bhole, Vatsal Vora, Nishit Solanki and Teja Naidu as students of AnantU had the opportunity to participate in a cultural exchange workshop titled, ‘ Undivided Identities: Unknown Stories of the Partition’ organised by Bangalore-based organisation, ReReeti Foundation for Museums, along with 10 students of Pakistan from National College of Arts, Pakistan.

The workshop delved into the experiences of the migrants of both countries at the time of the famously tumultuous Partition in 1947, and through this, explored the idea and significance of an ‘identity’. In the process, they also discovered the evident chasm between their identity according to their country (aka the national identification documents) and their identity according to themselves (the objects they consider precious; objects that speak of their personality, such as a beloved heirloom or a cherished artwork).

Over the course of the virtual workshop, with the partition of British India as the backdrop, the participants were encouraged to think over questions of what it means to belong to a nation, how they know (or decide? choose?) who “they” are, and the ways in which physical migration affects that knowledge. The participants also tried to understand and accept the ways in which historical currents have shaped them – and also, through that understanding, they were able to come to terms with their own identity and create newer, freer ways of relating with each other across the boundaries of country and region.

The course also included viewings of related material such as videos about people talking about their perceived identities and its significance to them, as well as poems and stories related to the Partition. The poem ‘Partition’ by W. H. Auden and the story ‘Toba Tek Singh’ by Saadat Hasan Manto were 2 pieces of literature that were discussed extensively during the course.

In addition to the discussions on ‘identity’, the workshop also touched upon the topic of ‘climate change’ and how the long-held grudge between India and Pakistan, and for that matter, other nations in similar positions, should be set aside in the light of global concerns.

This workshop was conducted by Sonali Sathaye, an anthropologist with a deep interest in education, social justice, and preserving the natural world, and Akanksha Maglani, a designer and educator whose research and practice lie at the intersection of the evolution and experience of the museum as an institution, and its purpose in a cross-cultural frame.

Touchless Door Bell System: An Internationally Recognised Innovation

Previous Next
Ayush Deshpande, B.Des

AnantU student Suchit Thakkar published a paper on ‘Automatic Temperature Detection using Touchless Door Bell’ in International Research Journal of Engineering and Technology. Assisted by faculty members, Parth Shah and Milind Chitale and co-authored by Nikita Shah, Assistant Professor, Sal College of Engineering, the paper highlighted how people can enter commercial spaces using contactless doorbell systems.

The automated temperature and buzzing system contain sensors which will measure the person’s temperature at the entry point and allow admission if it is below the permissible level. The device, which operates by hand movements, will generate a green light upon detection of temperature and the doorbell will buzz automatically, allowing the person to enter without touching any surface, common walls or doors. If the temperature is high and the gate is not opening at that moment the guard or the house member will get to know that the person standing outside has a high temperature and has a fever or some problem within the body.

This system can be used at every possible entry point to check the body parameters. It can majorly be used in offices and crowded places where a maximum number of people are visiting in a single day.

Om Rane, B.Des | Saipriya Yellayi, B.Des

The ANUBOT follows an open-source design published as a DIY project under the name of ‘Otto’. The latest innovation ANUBOT, a cute dancing robot, an initiative of the Makerspace team, aimed at making people familiar with several aspects of design technology and the process.

The team considered various permutations and combinations of processes and techniques while creating this project. Such techniques involved 3D printing all of the required parts, assembling the robot, using an Arduino to drive it and programming the Arduino to make the robot. The parts themselves were printed from PLA and ABS filaments and the files from the otto project were tweaked to be compatible with these materials.

ANUBOT, following in the footsteps of Otto, can perform a plethora of actions from moonwalking, to detecting and avoiding obstacles when walking. One can even manually control it using a keyboard!

Saipriya Yellayi, B.Des

Makerspace, in line with AnantU’s core philosophy rooted in sustainability, has produced yet another project - clay pot air cooler - which derives its energy from renewable sources such as the sunlight and uses sustainable materials like clay pots.

The project ‘Green Solution’- A solar-powered air: A tabletop clay pot air cooler, welded materials such as clay pot, solar panel, tiny fan (cooling fan for pc) and simple wiring with the intention to demonstrate to the students, the effect of evaporative cooling while using a natural resource such as sunlight to drive something as simple as a fan. The project was completed in a few hours using only natural resources in terms of both material and energy. Moreover, the ample availability of the clay pots in warm regions like Ahmedabad was an added advantage that deserved to be exhibited.

Smart Bin

Previous Next
Saipriya Yellayi, B.Des

'Smart bin' is an innovative way to manage garbage systems during COVID-19 pandemic in hospitals, malls, religious and public places and offices. The smart bin opens automatically when the user approaches within its range thus keeping the disposal process contactless and hygienic. Additionally, the bin warns the garbage collectors when it reaches its capacity signalling that it needs to be emptied. This enables the garbage collection systems to function timely and prevent overflowing of garbage. Smart bin serves as a great alternative to the traditional system as it facilitates the cities in keeping areas free of Bio-Waste that could potentially be hazardous and spread infections.
